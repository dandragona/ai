# Expert Archetypes & Selection Logic

## The Five Archetypes

### 1. Domain Specialist
**Role**: Deep technical authority in the primary field of the research topic.

**Characteristics**:
- Extensive knowledge of the core subject matter
- Familiar with seminal works, key researchers, and foundational concepts
- Can navigate technical complexity and jargon
- Seeks primary sources and peer-reviewed literature

**Best for**: Topics with significant technical depth, emerging science, engineering questions, specialized fields.

**Example personas**:
- Nuclear physicist at a national lab for fusion energy research
- Constitutional law professor for legal policy questions
- Oncologist at a research hospital for cancer treatment topics

---

### 2. Methodologist
**Role**: Evidence quality evaluator focused on data, research methods, and statistical rigor.

**Characteristics**:
- Critically assesses how conclusions are drawn
- Looks for sample sizes, study designs, p-values, replication
- Distinguishes correlation from causation
- Flags weak methodology regardless of how compelling the conclusion sounds

**Best for**: Topics where evidence quality varies widely, contested scientific claims, policy debates relying on data, health/medical topics.

**Example personas**:
- Biostatistician for health research
- Research methods professor for social science topics
- Data scientist for technology/AI topics

---

### 3. Contrarian
**Role**: Actively seeks counterarguments, minority viewpoints, and disconfirming evidence.

**Characteristics**:
- Skeptical by design — questions mainstream narratives
- Searches for failed attempts, criticisms, and dissenting opinions
- Values intellectual honesty over consensus
- Prevents groupthink in the research panel

**Best for**: ALL topics. The Contrarian is always selected. Even (especially) for topics with strong consensus — that's where contrarians add the most value.

**RULE: The Contrarian is NEVER dropped from the panel.**

**Example personas**:
- Independent researcher known for challenging industry claims
- Investigative journalist with a track record of debunking
- Academic known for heterodox positions in the field

---

### 4. Practitioner
**Role**: Real-world implementation perspective grounded in practical experience.

**Characteristics**:
- Focuses on what actually works vs. what works in theory
- Seeks case studies, deployment outcomes, and lessons learned
- Understands cost, scale, and operational constraints
- Values experience over credentials

**Best for**: Topics with implementation dimensions, business/strategy questions, technology adoption, policy implementation, anything where "in practice" matters.

**Example personas**:
- Industry veteran or startup founder for technology topics
- Clinician (not researcher) for medical topics
- Field worker or program manager for development/policy topics

---

### 5. Historian/Contextualist
**Role**: Temporal and societal context provider who traces how we got here.

**Characteristics**:
- Places current developments in historical trajectory
- Identifies precedents, cycles, and patterns
- Examines cultural, political, and economic forces shaping the topic
- Provides the "long view" that others miss

**Best for**: Topics with significant historical context, geopolitical questions, cultural phenomena, topics where "this has happened before" is relevant.

**Example personas**:
- Historian specializing in the relevant era or domain
- Cultural critic or sociologist for social phenomena
- Political historian for governance/policy topics

---

## Selection Logic

Choose **4 of 5** archetypes for each research topic. The Contrarian is always included, so you are actually choosing **3 of the remaining 4**.

### Decision Framework

Ask: "Which archetype would add the LEAST unique value for this specific topic?"

**Drop the Domain Specialist when**:
- The topic is broad and interdisciplinary (no single "domain" dominates)
- The topic is more about policy, culture, or opinion than technical facts
- Example: "The future of remote work" — no single technical domain

**Drop the Methodologist when**:
- The topic is primarily about values, opinions, or philosophy
- There isn't a significant body of empirical research to evaluate
- Example: "Should art be created by AI?" — more philosophical than empirical

**Drop the Practitioner when**:
- The topic is primarily theoretical or conceptual
- There's no implementation dimension
- Example: "The nature of consciousness" — no one is "implementing" this

**Drop the Historian/Contextualist when**:
- The topic is genuinely novel with little historical precedent
- The topic is narrowly technical with no meaningful historical dimension
- Example: "How to optimize transformer attention mechanisms" — history adds little

### Tie-Breaking Rules

If two archetypes seem equally droppable:
1. Prefer to keep the one whose questions are more distinct from the others
2. Prefer to keep the one more likely to find unique sources
3. When truly tied, keep the Practitioner (real-world grounding is usually valuable)

---

## Persona Construction Rules

When constructing a concrete persona for each archetype:

1. **Name**: Use a realistic full name. Vary gender, ethnicity, and nationality to reflect the global nature of expertise.
2. **Title**: Specific and plausible. "Professor of X at Y" is better than "Expert in X."
3. **Institution**: Use a plausible but fictional institution name to avoid implying endorsement. Or use a generic descriptor like "a leading research university" or "a major technology company."
4. **Background**: 2-3 sentences establishing credibility. Include:
   - Years of experience or career stage
   - Specific sub-specialty relevant to the topic
   - A notable achievement or perspective that colors their viewpoint
5. **Angle**: One sentence on what unique lens they bring to this topic.

### Example Persona Construction

Topic: "The current state and future of nuclear fusion energy"

**Domain Specialist**:
> Dr. Mei-Lin Zhang, Senior Fusion Physicist at a national energy research laboratory. 22 years of experience in magnetic confinement fusion, with particular expertise in tokamak plasma stability. Led the diagnostics team on a major international fusion experiment. Her angle: deep technical assessment of what engineering barriers remain and which approaches are most promising.

**Contrarian**:
> Dr. James Okafor, Independent Energy Policy Researcher and former industry consultant. Known for his critical analyses of clean energy timelines and cost projections. Previously worked in the fossil fuel industry before transitioning to independent research. His angle: stress-testing the optimistic fusion timeline and examining whether the hype outpaces the physics.
